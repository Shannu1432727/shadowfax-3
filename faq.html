🦷 Tooth Pain & Sensitivity

Tooth pain without a visible cavity can be caused by several things: a cracked tooth (invisible to the naked eye), gum recession exposing the root, grinding teeth at night (bruxism), an old filling that has worn down, or even sinus pressure affecting the upper back teeth. Sensitivity to hot, cold, or biting pressure are common symptoms. We recommend visiting us as soon as possible — what feels like minor sensitivity can progress to a root canal situation if left untreated. A brief examination and X-ray can identify the cause accurately.

Intermittent tooth pain is actually a warning sign that should not be ignored. When a nerve is inflamed or partially infected, pain may come and go — but without treatment, it will eventually become constant and severe. Common causes include early pulpitis (nerve inflammation), a cracked filling, or a developing abscess. The earlier you seek treatment, the more conservative (and affordable) the solution. Please do not wait for the pain to become unbearable — book an appointment with us at the first sign.

🪥 Scaling & Cleaning Myths

This is one of the most common misconceptions about dental care. Scaling does NOT damage or loosen teeth. What many people feel after scaling is that their teeth feel "wobbly" — but this is because the tartar that was sitting between the gums and teeth was actually holding the teeth together abnormally. Once removed, you feel the natural slight mobility that every tooth has. In reality, professional cleaning removes the very bacteria that destroy gum attachments and cause teeth to loosen over time. Regular scaling is one of the best things you can do to keep your teeth for life.

For most people with good oral hygiene, professional cleaning every 6 months is ideal. If you have gum disease, diabetes, a tendency to form tartar quickly, smoke, or wear braces, we may recommend cleaning every 3–4 months. Children should also have their teeth cleaned professionally from age 2–3 to establish good habits early. Regular professional cleaning combined with correct home brushing and flossing is the foundation of lifelong oral health.

😁 Braces & Orthodontics

Absolutely not! While braces are most commonly associated with teenagers, adults of any age can successfully undergo orthodontic treatment. Teeth can be moved at any age as long as the gums and bone are healthy. In fact, adult orthodontics is one of the fastest-growing areas of dentistry. We treat patients in their 40s, 50s, and beyond. For adults who prefer discretion, we offer ceramic braces and clear aligners (invisible aligners) that are nearly undetectable. A consultation with our orthodontist will determine the best option for you.

Treatment time varies depending on the complexity of your case. Mild crowding or spacing may be corrected in 6–12 months. Moderate cases typically take 12–18 months. Complex cases involving significant bite correction may take 18–24 months or more. Compliance plays a huge role — patients who attend regular adjustment appointments, wear rubber bands as instructed, and maintain good oral hygiene tend to finish treatment on the shorter end of the estimate. We will give you a realistic timeline at your initial consultation.

🔩 Dental Implants

Dental implants offer several significant advantages. They are permanent — fused to the jawbone, they don't slip or require removal. They preserve bone health — unlike dentures, implants stimulate the bone and prevent the facial collapse that occurs with tooth loss. They are the most natural-feeling replacement — you eat, speak, and smile with complete confidence. They don't damage adjacent teeth — unlike bridges, no healthy tooth structure is removed. And with proper care, implants can last a lifetime. While the upfront cost is higher than dentures, they often prove more economical over time.

Most healthy adults are candidates for implants. The key requirements are adequate jawbone volume to support the implant and healthy gums. If you have experienced bone loss (common after long-term tooth loss), bone grafting procedures can restore sufficient volume before implant placement. Conditions like controlled diabetes or certain medications require assessment but do not automatically disqualify you. Smoking significantly reduces implant success rates and we strongly advise quitting before and after implant surgery. A 3D cone-beam CT scan at our clinic will accurately assess your bone structure and suitability.

💡 Oral Hygiene Tips

Use a soft-bristled toothbrush and fluoride toothpaste. Hold the brush at a 45-degree angle to your gum line. Use gentle, circular or short back-and-forth strokes — never aggressive scrubbing which can damage gums and enamel. Brush for a full 2 minutes, twice a day — morning and before bed. Make sure to brush every surface: outer, inner, and chewing surfaces of all teeth, plus your tongue. Replace your toothbrush every 3 months or sooner if the bristles are frayed. An electric toothbrush is an excellent upgrade — they are clinically proven to remove more plaque.

Flossing is critically important — it cleans approximately 35% of tooth surfaces that your toothbrush simply cannot reach. Gum disease and cavities between teeth are almost entirely preventable with regular flossing. Use about 45cm of floss, wrap most around your middle fingers, and use your index fingers and thumbs to guide it. Gently slide it between each tooth in a C-shape against the tooth surface, going just below the gum line. Use a fresh section of floss for each tooth. If you find traditional floss difficult, try floss picks or a water flosser — both are effective alternatives. Floss once daily, ideally at night.

Foods that contribute most to tooth decay and gum disease include: sugary snacks and sweets (especially sticky ones like toffee and dried fruit that cling to teeth), carbonated soft drinks and sports drinks (highly acidic, erode enamel), citrus fruits in excess, white bread and refined carbohydrates that break down to sugar, and ice (chewing ice cracks teeth). Alcohol and coffee in excess can dry the mouth and stain teeth. On the positive side, dairy products, leafy greens, nuts, crunchy vegetables, and plenty of water all benefit oral health. If you do consume sugar, rinse with water immediately — and never sip on sweet drinks over long periods.
